My Journey to Linux: From BBS to Debian

In the early 80's, I first encountered the nascent personal computer scene at the Meadowlands Community Center's monthly show. It quickly turned into an IBM Clone Marketplace, but I was already devoted to the Commodore ecosystem, owning a Vic20, C64, C128, A500, A2000, A2500, and two A3000 Video Toasters. My Amiga 2000's superior video capabilities made the expensive IBM clones running Windows 3.1 look primitive and slow.

Despite my allegiance, I eventually acquired a Leading Edge 386 clone with a 40MB hard drive, a Sound Blaster, and an expensive graphics card. It still felt horrible compared to the Amiga, but the shift was undeniable.

My focus quickly moved to running a BBS on a Commodore 64, backed by four disk drives and two dedicated phone lines. My system was cutting-edge for the time, featuring voice control using the Covox Voice digitizer, along with audio and video digitizers.

The movement I found truly compelling wasn't the crippled "Public Domain" shareware, but the burgeoning Open Source concept. It was far superior, based on freedom and quality. My open-source software section quickly replaced the pirated games section on my BBS.

This early passion for control, quality, and community ultimately led me to the stability of Linux. I maintain a profound trust in Debian, which remains my core operating system today.
